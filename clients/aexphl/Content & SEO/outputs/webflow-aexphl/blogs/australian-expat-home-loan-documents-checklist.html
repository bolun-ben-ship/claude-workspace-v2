The Documents You Need for an Australian Expat Home Loan

TL;DR: The documents for an expat home loan are similar to a standard Australian loan application, with three additions: proof of foreign income, foreign tax documentation, and certified translations if your documents aren't in English. The most common reason applications stall isn't missing documents — it's poorly prepared ones. Here's what you need and what to watch out for.

One of the things I hear from clients a lot: they spent months working towards a property purchase, then the application stalled because a document was missing, outdated, or in the wrong format.

The Australian banking system isn't set up with overseas applicants in mind. That's not a complaint — it's just the reality. Documents that are straightforward for a Sydney-based borrower become more complex when your payslips are in SGD and your employer is a multinational headquartered in London.

This is the full list. Work through it before you start making offers.

Identity Documents

  • Australian passport — current. If it's expired, renew it first.
  • Foreign passport or visa if you hold dual nationality or a permanent residency visa for another country
  • Driver's licence — Australian preferred; some lenders accept foreign licences as secondary ID

Most lenders require 100 points of Australian ID. Your Australian passport alone is typically sufficient. If yours is expired and you're in the early stages of planning, sort this first — it affects everything downstream including conveyancing and settlement.

Income Documents

This is where it gets more involved for expats.

Salaried employees

  • Last 3 months of payslips from your overseas employer — in English, or with certified translation
  • Employment contract — showing base salary, role, and employer. If your contract is multi-year, that helps.
  • Employment letter confirming current employment, role, and salary — dated within 3 months
  • Last 3 months of bank statements showing salary credits — from both your foreign account and any Australian accounts

The payslips and employment letter are the most important. Some lenders want 6 months of payslips rather than 3 — worth asking your broker which applies to your target lender.

If your income includes bonus, allowances, or RSUs

  • Bonus history — last 2 years of bonus payments on payslips or a separate bonus letter from employer
  • RSU vesting schedule or brokerage statements showing recent vesting
  • Allowance breakdown in your employment contract — housing, car, education allowances if applicable

Not all lenders count all of these. The documents you need to provide for bonus income are the same regardless — whether the lender uses them is a separate question.

Tax Documents

  • Last 2 years of Australian tax returns — if you've been filing in Australia. Non-residents may have minimal Australian tax obligations, but the returns (even showing nil income) can be useful for continuity of identity.
  • Last 2 years of overseas tax returns or tax assessments — required by most lenders to verify income history. Singapore's Notice of Assessment works for this. HK has an equivalent.
  • Foreign tax residency certificate if applicable — confirms you're a registered taxpayer in your country of residence

If you haven't been filing Australian tax returns, you're not alone — many non-resident expats have minimal Australian taxable income. Most lenders will work with overseas tax documentation in lieu of Australian returns, but they need something showing your income history.

Certified Translations

Any document that isn't in English needs a certified translation by a NAATI-accredited translator (National Accreditation Authority for Translators and Interpreters). This applies to:

  • Foreign payslips
  • Employment contracts in a language other than English
  • Overseas tax documents
  • Foreign bank statements

Singapore's standard business language is English, so most payslips and employment contracts from Singapore-based employers are already in English. This is less of an issue for Singapore-based expats than it is for those in Hong Kong (Cantonese documents) or parts of the Middle East.

Property Documents (if you've already identified a property)

  • Contract of sale — once you have one signed
  • Copy of title — your conveyancer can get this
  • Building and pest inspection reports if applicable
  • Strata report if purchasing an apartment

If you're at the pre-approval stage (which is where you should start), you don't need property documents yet. Pre-approval is based on your financial position, not a specific property.

Australian Financial Documents

  • Last 3–6 months of Australian bank statements showing savings and any existing loan repayments
  • Statements for any existing Australian loans — investment properties, personal loans, credit cards
  • Evidence of deposit funds — savings statements going back 3 months to show genuine savings (not a gift that appeared last week)

If your deposit is being funded from overseas savings, you'll need bank statements from your foreign account showing the accumulation of funds. A lump sum transfer without history raises questions.

Power of Attorney (sometimes required)

Some lenders require a Power of Attorney if you won't be present in Australia at settlement. This authorises someone in Australia — a family member, solicitor, or conveyancer — to sign documents on your behalf.

Not all lenders require this, and the requirement varies by state. Your conveyancer will advise. If you're using a solicitor for conveyancing, they can often act as attorney as part of the same engagement.

What Causes Applications to Stall

Honestly, it's rarely a missing document. It's usually one of these:

  • Employment letter is too old — lenders want it dated within 3 months. If you got one six months ago, it needs refreshing.
  • Bank statements don't show salary credits clearly — if your salary goes to a foreign account and then gets transferred to a different account, the trail needs to be clear.
  • Bonus income claimed without documentation history — one bonus on one payslip doesn't count. You need consistency across at least two years.
  • Certified translations missing on non-English documents — easily fixed, but it delays things.
  • Deposit funds not demonstrably "genuine savings" — lenders want to see the accumulation, not just the arrival.

FAQ

What are the key documents needed?

Passport, 3 months of overseas payslips, employment contract, employment letter, 2 years of overseas tax returns, 3–6 months of bank statements, and certified translations if documents aren't in English.

Do I need NAATI certified translations?

Only for non-English documents. Most Singapore-based payslips and contracts are in English, so this is less commonly required for Singapore expats.

Do I need to be in Australia at settlement?

No. A Power of Attorney allows someone in Australia to sign on your behalf. Your conveyancer can usually act as attorney.

What if I haven't filed Australian tax returns?

Most lenders accept overseas tax documentation (e.g. Singapore's Notice of Assessment) for non-residents with minimal Australian income.
